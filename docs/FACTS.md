## The thesis this substantiates
**"It's not the diabetes that kills you — it's the infection."** The facts below show both halves:
*why* a diabetic wound gets infected (the immune failure) and *what infection creates* (the cascade).
And the answer to both is the same: **catch it early — keep the foot.**

---

## WHO IT'S FOR — Type 1 & Type 2 (inclusive)
- **Type 1 or Type 2 — if you live with diabetes, your feet carry the risk.** *(positioning)*
- Type 1 is **autoimmune** (the immune system destroys insulin-making cells); Type 2 is **insulin
  resistance** — but both damage the nerves and circulation in the feet. Type 2 has higher ulcer
  *prevalence*, but **after a foot ulcer, Type 1 is not spared** — the risk of amputation-or-death is
  actually *higher* in Type 1 (adjusted HR **2.09** vs 1.65). — *Diabetes Care, 2022*

## The two landing-page `[cite]` anchors (The Stakes)
- **[cite 1]** Diabetic nerve damage can dull the feeling in the feet — a cut or sore can form with
  **no pain to warn you.** — *NIDDK / NIH*
- **[cite 2]** **Most diabetes-related amputations begin as a foot ulcer** — one that, caught early,
  is often treatable. — *Diabetes Care, 2023*

## High risk is real math (clinical burden)
- Up to **half** of people with diabetes develop nerve damage in their feet. — *NIDDK/NIH; Diabetes Care 2023*
- **19–34%** of people with diabetes will develop a foot ulcer in their lifetime. — *Diabetes Care 2023*
- Once healed, a foot ulcer **recurs in ~40% within 1 year** (65% within 3). — *IWGDF 2023 / NEJM 2017*
- Five years after a diabetic foot ulcer, survival is **comparable to many cancers**. — *Armstrong et al., J Foot Ankle Res 2020 (pooled estimate)*

## The immune "why" (the mechanism — established mechanism)
- High blood sugar **slows the immune cells that rush to a wound**, leaving a diabetic foot vulnerable
  to infection right when it's most fragile (delayed neutrophil chemotaxis → early "window of
  vulnerability"). — *eLife 2022*
- In diabetes, wounds get **stuck in inflammation and don't heal normally** (impaired macrophage/
  neutrophil function) — how a small sore lingers and turns serious. — *Advances in Wound Care 2024; Int Wound J 2026*

## Infection — what it creates (the cascade)
- **About half of diabetic foot ulcers are already infected by the time they're seen** (40–56% across sources). — *IDSA/IWGDF, Clin Infect Dis 2023*
- Diabetic foot infections are the **most common diabetes complication that puts people in the hospital.** — *IDSA/IWGDF, Clin Infect Dis 2023*
- Up the ladder: once infection reaches the **bone (osteomyelitis), amputation odds rise ~4×** (OR 3.70);
  with **gangrene, ~11×** (OR 10.90). — *PLoS One 2020 meta-analysis (odds ratios)*

## Why prevention matters (catch it early)
- **Checking foot temperature at home cut new ulcers roughly in half** across five clinical trials
  (pooled RR **0.51**, 95% CI 0.31–0.84). — *pooled meta-analysis 2022 / IWGDF 2023* (certainty moderate-to-low)
- In early trials, daily foot-temperature checks dropped ulcers **20% → 2%**. — *Lavery, Diabetes Care 2004 (small trial)*
- **Multidisciplinary foot-care teams reduced major amputations in 94% of studies reviewed.** — *Musuuza et al., J Vasc Surg 2020*
- **Relieving pressure — offloading (total contact cast) — is the cornerstone of healing** a foot ulcer. — *IWGDF 2023*

## Cost (label the year)
- About **one-third of the ~$237B** spent on U.S. diabetes care (2017) is tied to foot complications —
  on par with treating all cancers (~$80B, 2015). — *Armstrong et al. 2020 (estimate) / ADA 2017*

## ⚠️ DO NOT USE — adversarially refuted or unverified
- ❌ "Up to 85% of diabetic amputations are preventable" — **origin never verified** (likely older
  WHO/St. Vincent-era). Do not use until sourced.
- ❌ "20% of ulcer patients require amputation / ≥19% lifetime amputation risk" — **refuted** (0-3).
- ❌ "~60% 5-year mortality after a foot ulcer / only ~40% survive" — **refuted** (0-3).
- ❌ "Diabetic wounds carry 2–4 log more bacteria than normal wounds" — **refuted** (0-3).
- ❌ **Market / TAM / CAGR numbers** — market-research-firm figures did **not survive verification**
  in two passes (single-source, conflicting). Build the market case bottom-up from the **population
  base only, cited from primary sources** (CDC National Diabetes Statistics Report; IDF Diabetes
  Atlas), and label any analyst TAM as "estimate, ranges vary."

## Framing rules (keep it defendable)
- Immune-mechanism claims → "established mechanism," mechanistic language, no outcome guarantees.
- Bone/gangrene figures are **odds ratios**, not risk ratios.
- "Half of ulcers infected" spans 40–56% — keep the hedge.
- Cost figures carry their year (2017 US spend); the ⅓ is an estimate.
- Do not imply "Type 1 is safer" — both are high-risk; after an ulcer, Type 1's relative risk is higher.
